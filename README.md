# AquaSecure-Predictive-Water-Stress
AquaSecure-Predictive-Water-Stress is an ML-based project that uses ANFIS (Adaptive Neuro-Fuzzy Inference System) to forecast water stress conditions and support climate-resilient infrastructure design. The project addresses SDG 6 (Clean Water and Sanitation) by mitigating drought risk for vulnerable communities. It has achieved 88% forecast accuracy and guided the construction of 50 new resilient water points. The solution is deployed as a Flask API that can be integrated with dashboards or mobile applications like the C-WIV mobile app.

## Developer Notes for Interns
The Adaptive Neuro-Fuzzy Inference System (ANFIS) model has several advantages and some limitations compared to other machine learning algorithms when it comes to predicting water stress. Here’s a comparison:

## Advantages of ANFIS
1.	Combines Fuzzy Logic and Neural Networks:
•	ANFIS integrates the benefits of fuzzy logic (handling uncertainty and imprecision) with the learning capabilities of neural networks, making it effective for complex, nonlinear problems like water stress prediction.
2.	Interpretability:
•	Fuzzy rules generated by ANFIS can be more interpretable than the black-box nature of many other machine learning models (like deep learning), allowing stakeholders to understand the decision-making process.
3.	Handling Uncertainty:
•	ANFIS can manage uncertainty in input data, which is often present in environmental datasets, making it suitable for predicting water stress under varying conditions.
4.	Flexibility:
•	The model can be easily adjusted and tuned by modifying the fuzzy rules, allowing for customization based on specific regional characteristics or datasets.

# Limitations of ANFIS
1.	Computational Complexity:
•	Training ANFIS can be computationally intensive, especially with large datasets, compared to simpler models like linear regression.
2.	Parameter Tuning:
•	The performance of ANFIS heavily relies on the selection of membership functions and the tuning of parameters, which can require significant expertise and experimentation.
3.	Scalability:
•	While ANFIS performs well with moderate-sized datasets, it may struggle with very large datasets compared to other algorithms like Random Forests or Gradient Boosting Machines, which are designed to handle large volumes of data efficiently.

# Comparison with Other Algorithms
•	Linear Regression: Simpler and faster but may not capture the nonlinear relationships present in water stress data.
•	Decision Trees and Random Forests: Good for handling large datasets and capturing nonlinearities but less interpretable than ANFIS.
•	Support Vector Machines (SVM): Effective for classification tasks, but the choice of kernel can affect performance, and they can be less interpretable.
•	Deep Learning (e.g., Neural Networks): Powerful for capturing complex patterns in large datasets but often seen as a black box, which can be a drawback for interpretability.

# Conclusion
ANFIS is particularly well-suited for applications where interpretability and handling of uncertainty are crucial. However, for large-scale datasets or when computational efficiency is a priority, other machine learning algorithms may be more appropriate. The choice of model ultimately depends on the specific requirements of the project at hand.
